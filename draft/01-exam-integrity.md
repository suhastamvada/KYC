# Chapter 01 — Examination Integrity (NEET and national entrance exams)

The 2024 NEET-UG paper leak and its aftermath — court proceedings, a
government-appointed expert committee, and the Public Examinations
(Prevention of Unfair Means) Act, 2024 — exposed structural weaknesses in how
India conducts high-stakes national examinations. This chapter consolidates
reforms addressing them.

---

## 1.1 Transition NEET-UG to secure computer-based testing

**Problem.** A single-day, pen-and-paper exam for 2M+ candidates requires
printing and physically transporting question papers to thousands of centres,
creating a long custody chain where a single compromised link leaks the paper
nationally.

**Evidence.** The 2024 leak propagated through the physical custody chain.
JEE (Main), of comparable scale, runs computer-based in multiple shifts with
no equivalent leak event. *(Citations to be added: CBI charge sheet
reporting; committee recommendations.)*

**Proposed reform.** Phase NEET-UG to multi-session computer-based testing
with per-session item generation from a calibrated item bank and statistical
score equating across sessions, following the JEE (Main) operational model.
Implementation: NTA, with an independent psychometric audit of the equating
method published annually.

**Tradeoffs.** Rural centre and connectivity gaps risk disadvantaging exactly
the candidates the exam should protect — requires large-scale centre buildout
first. Item-bank quality becomes the new single point of failure. Multi-shift
equating invites fairness disputes unless the method is published and audited.

**Affected parties.** ~2.3M annual candidates; NTA; test-centre operators;
state health-education departments.

---

## 1.2 Independent security audit and public incident reporting for the NTA

**Problem.** The agency that conducts the exam also investigates and reports
on its own failures, so the public learns of breaches through leaks and
litigation rather than disclosure.

**Evidence.** Timeline of the 2024 events: irregularities surfaced through
media and petitions before official acknowledgment. *(Citations to be added:
Supreme Court hearing record; press coverage timeline.)*

**Proposed reform.** A statutory requirement for an annual third-party
security audit of examination processes (paper/item custody, digital
infrastructure, centre operations) with a published summary, plus a mandatory
public incident-disclosure rule with defined timelines, modeled on breach-
disclosure norms in financial regulation.

**Tradeoffs.** Disclosure rules can incentivize under-classification of
incidents; audit quality depends on auditor independence and rotation. Adds
cost and administrative load to every cycle.

**Affected parties.** NTA; Ministry of Education; candidates and parents
(primary beneficiaries of disclosure).

---

## 1.3 Reduce single-attempt stakes via score portability

**Problem.** A single annual attempt concentrates catastrophic stakes on one
day, which fuels both the coaching arms race and the incentive to cheat: the
value of a leaked paper is proportional to the finality of the exam.

**Evidence.** Comparative practice: SAT/ACT multiple sittings; JEE (Main)
twice-yearly attempts with best-score counting. *(Citations to be added:
committee discussion of multi-session NEET.)*

**Proposed reform.** Conduct NEET-UG at least twice yearly with the best
valid score usable for that admission cycle, once computer-based delivery
(§1.1) makes multi-session operation feasible.

**Tradeoffs.** More sessions multiply operational surface area and cost.
Multiple attempts may advantage candidates who can afford repeated
preparation cycles unless fee waivers accompany the change.

**Affected parties.** Candidates; NTA; medical-college admission counselling
authorities (timeline compression).
